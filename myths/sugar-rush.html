The Common Belief

Many parents swear that sugar makes their children hyperactive. It's such a widespread belief that it's become a cultural touchstone, especially at birthday parties and holidays.

The Scientific Reality

Multiple double-blind studies have found no evidence that sugar causes hyperactivity in children. The most notable study, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, even found that children whose parents believed they were sensitive to sugar showed no behavioral changes when given sugar-rich foods.

Quick Facts:

  • No scientific evidence links sugar to hyperactivity
  • Parent expectations may influence how they perceive their children's behavior
  • Excitement at parties and events is often mistaken for "sugar rush"
  • The "sugar high" is largely a placebo effect

Why Do People Believe This?

The misconception likely stems from the contexts in which children typically consume lots of sugar - parties, holidays, and other exciting events. The excitement of these situations, not the sugar, causes the hyperactive behavior.

"The sugar-hyperactivity myth is one of the most persistent misconceptions in child nutrition, despite clear scientific evidence to the contrary." - Dr. Mark Wolraich, pediatrics researcher
